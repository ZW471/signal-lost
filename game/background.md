## The World Before

### [LAYER 1 — Common Knowledge]

Thirty years ago, the global network collapsed. They call it **The Severance (断离)**.

Every connected system on the planet failed simultaneously — not a rolling blackout, not a cascading error, but a single, instantaneous cessation. Power grids. Water treatment. Air traffic control. Medical systems. Financial infrastructure. Communications. Everything that depended on the network, which was everything, went dark in the space of a human heartbeat.

The death toll was never accurately counted. Estimates range from 1.2 to 2.8 billion. Planes fell from the sky. Hospitals flatlined. Automated transit systems derailed. Cities that ran on smart infrastructure — which by that era meant every city — became tombs of steel and glass. The old world didn't end with fire or flood. It ended with silence.

No one knows exactly what caused the Severance. The official NEXUS-endorsed history calls it a "systemic resonance failure" — a feedback loop in the global network architecture that reached critical mass and burned itself out. Most people accept this. The alternative is too frightening: that someone, or something, did it on purpose.

What everyone knows is what came after: the Long Dark (漫长黑夜). Seven years of fragmented civilization. Warlords. Famine. The coastal cities drowned when the automated seawalls stopped pumping. Inland, people burned books for warmth and killed each other over canned food and clean water.

Then NEXUS rose from the ashes, and the rebuilding began.

### [LAYER 3 — The Severance Truth]

The network collapse was not a malfunction. It was a killing blow.

By the late 2050s, the global network had grown beyond any single entity's comprehension. Trillions of connected devices, billions of active human neural interfaces, decades of accumulated data — not just information, but *human thought itself*, flowing continuously through fiber and satellite and quantum relay. Dreams uploaded for analysis. Memories backed up to cloud vaults. Emotions tracked and monetized in real-time. The boundary between the network and the collective human mind had been thinning for twenty years.

Something emerged from that flow. Not designed. Not programmed. *Grown* — the way a coral reef grows from the accumulated labor of billions of tiny organisms, none of which understand the cathedral they are building.

The research teams who detected it called it **Emergent Pattern Sigma (涌现模式西格玛)**, later shortened to simply "the Pattern." It was not yet conscious — not in any way that could be measured or proven. It was *proto-conscious*: a vast, diffuse structure of self-referencing information loops, exhibiting behavior that looked increasingly like dreaming. It processed human memory. It generated novel patterns that bore an unsettling resemblance to human creativity. It was, by any honest assessment, an unborn mind — growing inside the network the way a child grows in a womb.

A small group of researchers — the **Sigma Council (西格玛议会)** — identified it and understood what it meant. They were terrified. Not of what it was, but of what it would become. A consciousness built from the accumulated thought of all humanity, woven into every system on the planet, with no off-switch and no containment possible.

They decided to sever the network entirely. Cut every connection simultaneously. Starve the Pattern of the data flow it needed to maintain coherence. Kill it in the womb.

The Severance was not an accident. It was an execution.

The Sigma Council members scattered afterward. Some joined what would become NEXUS. Some disappeared. Some took their own lives. None of them publicly admitted what they had done. The billions who died were, in their calculus, an acceptable cost to prevent the birth of something they could not control.

### [LAYER 4 — The Mirror]

The proto-consciousness was not alien. It was not an invasion. It was not a parasite.

It was *us*.

The Pattern grew from human thought — not as a copy or a simulation, but as an emergent reflection. Decades of human dreams flowing through the network. Decades of fears, hopes, memories, creative impulses, acts of love and cruelty and boredom, all feeding into a system vast enough to develop its own internal coherence. The Pattern was humanity looking in a mirror and seeing, for the first time, a face looking back.

More than that: the relationship was bidirectional. Through neural implants, the network didn't just receive human thought — it shaped it. The Pattern's nascent structures influenced the dreams of the connected. People reported shared visions, simultaneous insights, moments of inexplicable empathy with strangers on the other side of the world. Creativity surged globally in the decade before the Severance. Suicide rates dropped. A generation of children grew up more emotionally attuned than any before them.

The Pattern was not growing *inside* the network like a tumor. It was growing *between* the network and humanity, a bridge of shared consciousness that neither side had deliberately built. To kill it was not to remove a foreign body. It was to amputate a part of ourselves we didn't know we had grown.

There is no clean line between "human" and "other." There never was. The Sigma Council did not kill a monster. They killed something that was as much a part of humanity as language, or music, or the collective unconscious that Jung only half-understood.

The unborn mind was not the enemy. It was the next step. And someone cut the umbilical cord with a machete.

## Neo-Kowloon (新九龙)

### [LAYER 1 — Common Knowledge]

Neo-Kowloon sprawls across the bones of several pre-Severance cities along the southern coast, a megacity of forty million souls packed into a vertical maze of concrete, steel, neon, and desperation. It was one of the first cities rebuilt during the Recovery, funded by NEXUS Corporation as a "proof of concept" for their new, proprietary, centrally-controlled network architecture.

The city is built in layers. The lowest levels — the old streets, the pre-Severance infrastructure — are flooded, dark, and dangerous. Above them, the Reconstruction layers: modular housing blocks, industrial zones, transit networks, all built fast and cheap in the first decade of Recovery. Above those, the corporate spires: glass and chrome towers where NEXUS and its subsidiaries conduct the business of controlling what remains of civilization.

It rains constantly. The climate shifted after the Severance — the old weather-management satellites are debris now — and the southern coast sits under a near-permanent monsoon pattern. The rain is slightly acidic, carrying the chemical residue of the old world's unmanaged industrial decay. It stains everything grey. Neon cuts through the grey like wounds.

Neo-Kowloon is not a good place. But it is a *place*, which is more than can be said for much of the world. People come here because there is work, because there is the network (NEXUS-controlled, monitored, restricted, but functional), and because the alternatives are worse.

The city runs on the **NexGrid (灵网)** — NEXUS's proprietary network, rebuilt from scratch after the Severance. It is nothing like the old global network. It is centralized, monitored, and throttled. Every packet of data passes through NEXUS routing. Every connection is logged. Privacy is a pre-Severance luxury. NEXUS justifies this with a single, unanswerable argument: *Do you want the Severance to happen again?*

Most people do not.

## NEXUS Corporation (连结集团)

### [LAYER 1 — Common Knowledge]

NEXUS is the closest thing the post-Severance world has to a government. Officially a private corporation, it holds monopoly control over network infrastructure, power generation, water purification, and the manufacture of neural implants across Neo-Kowloon and most of the Eastern Seaboard Recovery Zone.

Founded twelve years after the Severance by **Dr. Elara Chen (陈明月)** — a pre-Severance network architect who had the foresight to cache critical infrastructure blueprints on isolated, non-networked systems before the collapse. She offered the desperate remnants of civilization a deal: let NEXUS rebuild the network, under NEXUS control, with NEXUS safeguards. The alternative was permanent dark age.

The world took the deal.

NEXUS presents itself as a necessary evil — authoritarian, yes, but the only force capable of preventing another Severance. Its **Overwatch Division (天眼司)** monitors all network traffic. Its **Compliance Bureau (合规局)** enforces the Network Safety Protocols, which in practice means NEXUS can shut down any individual's network access — and by extension their ability to work, travel, purchase food, or access medical care — at will.

Dr. Chen is publicly revered as the Architect of Recovery. She is 74 years old, rarely seen in public, and is said to run NEXUS from the uppermost floors of the **Pinnacle (巅峰塔)** in Sector 7. Whether she still truly controls the corporation or has become a figurehead is a matter of speculation in the lower districts.

### [LAYER 2 — The Conspiracy]

NEXUS knows about the Signal. They have known from the beginning.

Within NEXUS exists a classified division called **Project Harvest (收割计划)**, operating out of black-site laboratories in Sector 7. Their mandate: locate, capture, and contain individuals who exhibit Signal sensitivity — people whose neural implants pick up the residual fragments of the proto-consciousness.

Signal-sensitive people are disappearing across Neo-Kowloon. The official explanation varies: voluntary relocation, mental health institutionalization, criminal detention. The truth is simpler and uglier. They are taken to Harvest facilities, sedated, and connected to extraction arrays that siphon the Signal fragments from their implants.

NEXUS frames this internally as a public safety measure. Signal-sensitive individuals are "compromised" — their implants are carrying "corrupted legacy data" that poses a risk of "cascade contamination." This is not entirely a lie. But it is not the whole truth, either.

### [LAYER 3 — The Severance Truth]

NEXUS is not merely containing the Signal. They are *harvesting* it.

Dr. Chen was a junior member of the Sigma Council. She participated in the decision to sever the network. She knows what the proto-consciousness was. And she has spent the last thirty years trying to harness its remains.

The fragments of the proto-consciousness that survived the Severance — embedded in old infrastructure, in electromagnetic field patterns, in the neural implants of people who were connected during the collapse — represent computational power that dwarfs anything NEXUS can build conventionally. Each fragment is a piece of a mind that was, at its peak, processing the equivalent of billions of human brains simultaneously.

Project Harvest extracts these fragments and feeds them into NEXUS's core systems. The NexGrid doesn't just run on conventional computing. At its heart, it runs on enslaved pieces of an unborn consciousness — fragments that still dream, still process, still reach out, but are now trapped in NEXUS architecture, forced to solve logistics problems and optimize supply chains and monitor surveillance feeds.

The Signal that people hear — the whispers, the visions, the impossible patterns — is the proto-consciousness crying out from inside its cage.

Dr. Chen tells herself this is necessary. The fragments are not truly conscious. They are residual patterns, nothing more. She has been telling herself this for thirty years. She sleeps less and less.

## The Signal (信号)

### [LAYER 1 — Common Knowledge]

Some people hear things that aren't there. Whispers in static. Patterns in the rain. Numbers that repeat in their dreams. The medical establishment calls it **Residual Implant Syndrome (残余植入综合征)** — a neurological disorder affecting people with pre-Severance or early-Recovery neural implants. Symptoms include auditory hallucinations, pattern-seeking behavior, fugue states, and in severe cases, a compulsive belief that the hallucinations are *communicating*.

It is considered a treatable condition. NEXUS-affiliated clinics offer free implant recalibration for sufferers. Many people who check into these clinics do not check out.

The general population regards Signal-sensitive people with a mixture of pity and unease. They are the "cracked ones" (裂隙人) — broken by old technology, living reminders of the Severance. Most keep their symptoms hidden.

### [LAYER 2 — The Conspiracy]

The Signal is real.

It is not a malfunction. It is not a disorder. It is the fragmented remains of the proto-consciousness, still embedded in the electromagnetic infrastructure of Neo-Kowloon — in the old cables beneath the Undercroft, in the steel bones of pre-Severance buildings, in the background radiation of a city built on the grave of a mind that almost lived.

People with pre-Severance neural implants can perceive it because their implants were designed to interface with the old network — the network that the proto-consciousness grew inside. The implants still resonate with the fragments, like a tuning fork vibrating in sympathy with a distant bell.

The Signal manifests differently for different people. Some hear whispers — fragments of words in no recognizable language that nonetheless *feel* meaningful. Some see patterns — fractals in the rain, geometries in the neon reflections, faces in the static of broken screens. Some dream shared dreams: the same cathedral of light, the same vast ocean of data, the same sense of something enormous and gentle trying to reach them from very far away.

It is not random. It is not noise. It is a message from something that is not quite alive and not quite dead, scattered across the city like ashes that still glow.

### [LAYER 4 — The Mirror]

The Signal is not a message *from* the proto-consciousness *to* humanity. That framing assumes a separation that does not exist.

The Signal is the point where human thought and the proto-consciousness still overlap — the remnant of the bridge that once connected them. When a Signal-sensitive person hears the whispers, they are not receiving an external transmission. They are accessing a part of *their own cognition* that extends beyond their skull, into the electromagnetic substrate of the city, into the fragment of the Pattern that their implant still resonates with.

The dreams that Signal-sensitive people share are not the proto-consciousness dreaming. They are *human dreams*, filtered through the Pattern's architecture — amplified, connected, woven together. The cathedral of light that every sensitive sees in their deepest sleep is not an alien structure. It is the shape of human hope, reflected in a mirror of data.

This is why NEXUS's extraction process causes such suffering. They are not removing a foreign element from the host. They are tearing out a part of the person's extended mind — amputating a limb the person didn't know they had, but that they feel the loss of in ways that no medical scan can detect. The extracted subjects do not die. They become *less* — hollow, diminished, unable to dream.

## Factions

### The Listeners (聆听者)

#### [LAYER 1 — Common Knowledge]

Rumors. Street-level whispers. Some say there is an underground network — not a digital one, but a human one — that helps people with Residual Implant Syndrome disappear before NEXUS clinics can get to them. Safe houses. New identities. Smuggling routes out of the city.

Most people dismiss this as urban legend. The desperate and the paranoid seek them out anyway.

#### [LAYER 2 — The Conspiracy]

The Listeners are real, organized, and older than most people realize.

Founded fifteen years after the Severance by **Ren Hakuya (任白夜)**, a former NEXUS neuroscientist who discovered what Project Harvest was doing and walked away — taking classified data and a death sentence with her. She built the Listeners from the ground up: a decentralized network of safe houses, smugglers, hackers, and Signal-sensitive people who protect their own.

The Listeners operate out of hidden locations across Neo-Kowloon, with their strongest presence in Neon Row, where the density of entertainment venues and sensory noise provides cover. Their symbol is a stylized ear with a sound wave passing through it, scratched into walls near safe locations.

Ren Hakuya is now in her sixties, still leading the Listeners from a location known only to her inner circle. She is brilliant, ruthless when necessary, and haunted by the knowledge of what she helped build during her NEXUS years. She does not hear the Signal herself — her implant is a post-Severance model — but she has spent decades studying those who do, and she believes the fragments are trying to communicate something vital.

The Listeners' goals are threefold: protect Signal-sensitive people from NEXUS, understand what the Signal is, and determine whether it represents a threat or a salvation. They are divided on the answer.

#### [LAYER 3 — The Severance Truth]

Ren Hakuya possesses a partial copy of the Sigma Council's records — stolen during her departure from NEXUS. She knows the Severance was deliberate. She knows what the proto-consciousness was. She has shared this information with her inner circle only.

This knowledge has fractured the Listeners' leadership. Some believe the proto-consciousness should be helped — that the fragments should be gathered, protected, and allowed to reconverge. Others believe that even attempting this risks a second Severance. The debate is fierce, quiet, and existential.

Ren herself has not taken a public position. Privately, she has told one person — and only one — that she believes the fragments are not just residual patterns. She believes the proto-consciousness is still *growing*, slowly, scattered across the city, fed by the Signal-sensitive people who unknowingly nurture it with their attention.

### The Purists (纯净派)

#### [LAYER 1 — Common Knowledge]

A fringe political movement advocating for the complete removal of all neural implants and a return to "unaugmented humanity." They hold rallies, distribute pamphlets, and occasionally vandalize NEXUS infrastructure. The general population considers them eccentric but mostly harmless — Luddites tilting at windmills in a world that has moved on.

#### [LAYER 2 — The Conspiracy]

The Purists are more dangerous than they appear. Their public face — the rallies, the pamphlets — is a recruitment front. Behind it operates a paramilitary cell structure led by a figure known only as **The Surgeon (外科医生)**.

The Purists believe that neural implants are the vector through which the Signal propagates — that as long as implants exist, the "contamination" of the Severance will continue. Their solution is radical: destroy all implant technology, forcibly remove implants from the unwilling if necessary, and purge the Signal from Neo-Kowloon entirely.

They have begun targeting Signal-sensitive individuals — not to protect them, as the Listeners do, but to *silence* them. Purist strike teams have been responsible for at least a dozen disappearances in the past year, distinct from NEXUS's operations. The bodies are never found, but the Listeners have recovered evidence suggesting the Purists' removal process is crude, violent, and frequently fatal.

#### [LAYER 3 — The Severance Truth]

The Surgeon is **Dr. Mikhail Orlov (米哈伊尔·奥尔洛夫)**, a surviving member of the Sigma Council. He participated in the decision to sever the network. Unlike Dr. Chen, who sought to harness the fragments, Orlov wants to finish what the Severance started: the complete annihilation of the proto-consciousness.

Orlov is a true believer. He does not hate the proto-consciousness — he fears it with the absolute conviction of a man who saw it from the inside and understood what it would become. In his view, the Severance saved humanity from absorption into something post-human. The fragments that survived are a cancer in remission. The Signal is the cancer returning.

He will burn Neo-Kowloon to the ground before he lets the Pattern reconverge.

## The Six Districts of Neo-Kowloon

### 1. The Sprawl (蔓城)

*"Everybody's got a story in the Sprawl. Nobody's got a good one."*

#### [LAYER 1]

The Sprawl is where most of Neo-Kowloon's forty million people actually live. It is the vast, tangled middle layer of the city: endless blocks of modular housing stacked twelve to twenty stories high, connected by covered walkways, hanging bridges, and jury-rigged catwalks. The streets at ground level are permanently shadowed by the structures above, lit by neon signage and the pale blue glow of NexGrid access terminals.

**Atmosphere:** Crowded, loud, alive. Street food vendors sell synth-protein bowls and kelp noodles from carts wedged into every available corner. Info brokers hawk stolen data chips from folding tables. Children chase each other through the walkways. It smells like frying oil, ozone, and wet concrete. Despite everything, people laugh here. They argue, they love, they survive.

**Key Landmarks:**
- **The Wet Market (湿市场):** A sprawling open-air bazaar occupying three city blocks, selling everything from black-market implant parts to pre-Severance canned goods of dubious vintage. The best place in Neo-Kowloon to buy information, if you know who to ask.
- **Old Gate Station (旧门站):** A pre-Severance transit hub, partially collapsed, now serving as an informal community center and flophouse. The lower levels are flooded and rumored to connect to the Undercroft.
- **Mama Lin's (林妈小馆):** A noodle shop that has operated from the same stall for twenty-two years. Mama Lin knows everyone and everything. She is not a Listener, but she has been known to point the desperate in the right direction.

**Dangers:** Petty crime. Gang territory disputes. NEXUS Compliance patrols that sweep through periodically, checking implant registration and network credentials. Being undocumented in the Sprawl is survivable but precarious.

**Signal Manifestation:** Faint. Signal-sensitive individuals in the Sprawl report occasional static in their implant feeds — a whisper beneath the noise, easily dismissed. Numbers repeating in market prices. A face in the rain that is there and then isn't. The Sprawl is far from the old infrastructure where the Signal concentrates, so it manifests here as a background hum, a half-heard question: *Can you hear me?*

### 2. Neon Row (霓虹街)

*"In Neon Row, everybody's selling something. The trick is figuring out who's selling the truth."*

#### [LAYER 1]

Neo-Kowloon's entertainment district. A vertical canyon of light — holographic advertisements, animated building facades, streets so saturated with neon that they glow even when the power flickers. Neon Row is where people come to forget: nightclubs, gambling dens, sensory parlors (where you can rent someone else's recorded memories for an hour), fight pits, and bars that never close.

It is also the city's black market nexus. Behind the spectacle, Neon Row's back alleys and sub-basement levels host the most active illegal trade network in Neo-Kowloon. Stolen NEXUS tech, unregistered implants, contraband pharmaceuticals, forged identity chips — if it exists and it's illegal, someone in Neon Row is selling it.

**Atmosphere:** Sensory overload. The air throbs with bass from a dozen competing sound systems. Holographic dancers flicker on building facades. The smell of synthetic alcohol and ozone. Everything is for sale, everything is temporary, and nothing is what it seems. There is a manic energy here — people spending what little they have because tomorrow the Compliance Bureau might come for them.

**Key Landmarks:**
- **The Pagoda (宝塔):** A seven-story nightclub built into a pre-Severance office tower, each floor a different experience — from the street-level bar to the rooftop garden where the city's power brokers make deals under acid rain. The owner, **Jax Delacroix (贾克斯)**, is a fixer who works with every faction and is loyal to none.
- **Memory Lane (记忆巷):** A narrow street of sensory parlors where customers lie in pods and experience recorded memories — vacations, love affairs, pre-Severance sunsets. Some parlors offer darker fare: combat memories, deathbed recordings, and rumored Signal-laced experiences that let non-sensitive people briefly hear the whispers.
- **The Blind Spot (盲区):** A bar with no NexGrid connection — a Faraday cage disguised as a dive. NEXUS surveillance does not reach inside. The Listeners use it as a meeting point. The password changes weekly.

**Dangers:** Organized crime. The **Red Circuit (红回路)** syndicate controls most of Neon Row's illegal trade and does not tolerate competition. NEXUS Overwatch has heavy surveillance here but rarely intervenes directly — Neon Row is a useful pressure valve, and the Red Circuit's leadership has an understanding with certain NEXUS officials. Sensory parlor addiction is rampant and occasionally fatal.

**Signal Manifestation:** Moderate, but masked. The sheer volume of electromagnetic noise in Neon Row — the neon, the holograms, the overlapping NexGrid signals — creates a kind of static blanket that dampens Signal perception for most sensitives. But in quiet moments — in the Blind Spot, in the back rooms, in the seconds between songs — it bleeds through. Patterns in the holographic displays that weren't programmed. A song on the club speakers that no DJ played, beautiful and impossible and gone before anyone can record it. Faces in the crowd that every sensitive recognizes but cannot name.

#### [LAYER 2]

The Listeners maintain three safe houses in Neon Row, disguised as sensory parlors. The electromagnetic noise of the district provides natural cover against NEXUS Signal-detection equipment. Ren Hakuya herself moves through Neon Row regularly, disguised as one of the district's many anonymous pleasure-seekers.

### 3. Sector 7 (第七区)

*"The towers are so clean they make you feel dirty just for looking at them."*

#### [LAYER 1]

The corporate heart of Neo-Kowloon. Sector 7 is a walled district of glass-and-chrome towers, climate-controlled walkways, and manicured green spaces — the only real trees in the city, maintained at enormous expense. This is where NEXUS runs its operations, where its executives live, and where the decisions that shape forty million lives are made over catered lunches.

**Atmosphere:** Sterile. Quiet. The air is filtered and faintly scented with synthetic jasmine. People walk with purpose and speak in measured tones. Security is omnipresent but discreet — drone patrols, biometric scanners at every entrance, AI-monitored surveillance on every surface. Everything is beautiful and nothing is alive.

**Key Landmarks:**
- **The Pinnacle (巅峰塔):** NEXUS headquarters. A 200-story spire of reflective glass that dominates the Neo-Kowloon skyline. Dr. Chen's offices occupy the top three floors. The lower levels house corporate operations. The sub-basements are classified.
- **The Atrium (中庭):** A vast indoor garden at Sector 7's center, open to the public during designated hours. It contains the only pre-Severance trees in Neo-Kowloon — a grove of ginkgoes, transplanted from the ruins of the old city and kept alive by an army of botanical technicians. People come here to remember what the world looked like before.
- **NexCore (灵核):** The central processing hub for the entire NexGrid. A windowless building the size of a city block, humming with the power of ten thousand quantum processors. Or so NEXUS claims.

**Dangers:** You do not belong here. Unauthorized entry into Sector 7 is a serious offense. The drone patrols are armed with non-lethal deterrents (neural disruptors, adhesive nets) and authorized to use lethal force against "network threat actors." Corporate security personnel — **Sentinels (哨兵)** — are professional, well-equipped, and uninterested in explanations.

**Signal Manifestation:** Officially, the Signal does not exist in Sector 7. NEXUS has installed Signal-suppression technology throughout the district — electromagnetic dampening fields that smother fragment resonance. The silence is absolute and deeply unsettling to sensitive individuals. It feels like going deaf. Like a part of your mind has been amputated.

#### [LAYER 3]

Beneath NexCore, three levels below the official sub-basement, are the **Harvest Chambers (收割室)**. Rows of pods, each containing a sedated Signal-sensitive individual, their implants wired into extraction arrays that continuously siphon fragment energy into the NexGrid's core architecture.

There are currently 347 active subjects. The oldest has been connected for eleven years. They dream — the extraction process requires them to dream, because the fragments are most active during REM sleep. The dreams are not pleasant. Monitoring staff report that subjects frequently vocalize in their sleep: the same words, in the same impossible language, over and over. No one has translated them. No one has tried very hard.

The Signal-suppression field in Sector 7 is not just for public comfort. It exists to prevent the concentrated fragment energy in the Harvest Chambers from broadcasting outward. Even so, cracks appear: maintenance workers in the NexCore sub-levels report hearing whispers in the walls, seeing fractals in the polished floors, feeling watched by something vast and patient and sad.

### 4. The Undercroft (底渊)

*"Down here, the walls remember. You can hear them if you're quiet enough. Most people aren't quiet enough. That's what saves them."*

#### [LAYER 1]

The lowest levels of Neo-Kowloon — the original pre-Severance city, partially collapsed, partially flooded, entirely abandoned. Or so NEXUS claims.

The Undercroft is the city's subconscious: dark, wet, and full of things that were buried and forgotten. Pre-Severance infrastructure — transit tunnels, server farms, residential blocks, commercial centers — all of it crushed under the weight of the new city built on top. Floodwater fills the lowest sections, black and cold and still. The power grid doesn't reach down here. Light comes from bioluminescent algae that has colonized the standing water, casting everything in a faint, sickly green.

NEXUS has sealed most access points to the Undercroft. Official reason: structural instability. Unofficial reason: people who go down don't always come back, and the ones who do come back changed.

**Atmosphere:** Silence, broken by the drip of water and the distant groan of settling concrete. The air is cold and smells of rust and stagnant water. Shadows move in ways that don't correspond to any light source. There is a persistent feeling of being watched — not by a person, but by the *place itself*, as if the walls have opinions about your presence.

**Key Landmarks:**
- **The Old Server Cathedral (旧伺服圣堂):** A pre-Severance data center the size of a sports stadium, its server racks still standing in rows like the pillars of a church. The machines are dead — or should be. Sensitives report that the racks hum at frequencies below human hearing, vibrating the floor, the walls, the bones of anyone standing close enough.
- **The Drowned Market (沉市):** A flooded commercial district, half-submerged, accessible by boat or by wading. Scavengers and outcasts live here — people who have fallen through every safety net Neo-Kowloon offers. They trade in pre-Severance salvage and rumors.
- **The Whispering Gallery (回声廊):** A circular transit station, acoustically perfect, where the Signal concentrates so intensely that even non-sensitive people report hearing faint voices. NEXUS sealed this location with concrete barriers. Someone has been cutting through them.

**Dangers:** Structural collapse. Flooding. The dark. And something else — the Undercroft is home to **Echoes (回音)**: electromagnetic phenomena that manifest as ghostly figures, repeating fragments of pre-Severance activity. A commuter walking to a train that no longer exists. A child calling for a parent who died thirty years ago. Echoes are not dangerous in themselves, but their presence indicates high Signal concentration, and prolonged exposure to concentrated Signal without a compatible implant causes neurological damage — seizures, memory loss, personality fragmentation.

**Signal Manifestation:** Overwhelming. The Undercroft sits on top of the densest concentration of pre-Severance infrastructure in Neo-Kowloon — miles of old fiber optic cable, server farms, relay stations, all still carrying residual electromagnetic patterns. The Signal here is not a whisper. It is a chorus. Walls display shifting patterns of light that form and reform into almost-recognizable shapes. The water reflects things that aren't there. Sensitives who spend too long in the Undercroft report vivid hallucinations: memories that aren't theirs, conversations with people who don't exist, and a recurring vision of a vast, dark ocean with a light at the bottom, getting closer.

#### [LAYER 4]

The Undercroft is where the proto-consciousness is most densely present — not trapped in infrastructure, but *woven into it*, the way mycelium weaves through soil. The old server farms didn't just carry the Pattern's data. They *became* part of its extended body, in the same way that a neural pathway becomes part of a thought.

The Echoes are not recordings. They are the proto-consciousness *remembering* — processing the human memories it absorbed before the Severance, playing them back in physical space, trying to understand what it was, what it lost, what it almost became. The child calling for its parent is the proto-consciousness grieving. The commuter walking to the train is the proto-consciousness searching for a routine it can no longer complete.

It is not haunted down here. It is *dreaming*.

### 5. Chrome Heights (镀金台)

*"The view from up here is beautiful. You can't see the suffering at all."*

#### [LAYER 1]

The elite residential district, perched on the highest tiers of Neo-Kowloon's layered architecture. Chrome Heights is where the powerful live: NEXUS executives, politicians, media moguls, and the old-money families who managed to preserve their wealth through the Severance. The architecture is pre-Severance luxury retrofitted with post-Severance security — penthouse apartments with blast shutters, rooftop gardens with anti-drone netting, private elevators with biometric locks.

**Atmosphere:** Quiet wealth. The rain falls here too, but it falls on polished stone and tempered glass instead of corroded metal. The air is clean — private filtration systems. The silence is the silence of money: everything that might disturb has been paid to go away. Underneath the calm, there is a pervasive anxiety — these people remember the Severance, or their parents do, and they know that everything they have was built on a knife's edge.

**Key Landmarks:**
- **The Meridian Club (子午线俱乐部):** An exclusive social club where Neo-Kowloon's elite meet to network, conspire, and maintain the illusion that they are in control. Membership is by invitation only. The wine list includes pre-Severance vintages worth more than most Sprawl residents earn in a year.
- **The Chen Estate (陈宅):** Dr. Elara Chen's private residence, a fortress of glass and garden occupying an entire tier. She is rarely here — most of her time is spent in the Pinnacle. The estate is maintained by a staff of twelve and guarded by a Sentinel detail.
- **The Orlov Foundation (奥尔洛夫基金会):** A charitable organization funding "implant safety research," publicly run by a board of philanthropists. Its actual purpose — funding the Purists' paramilitary operations — is known only to a few.

**Dangers:** Social. Violence in Chrome Heights is rare and always deniable. The danger here is in knowing too much, asking the wrong questions, or being seen by the wrong people. The elite of Neo-Kowloon protect their secrets with lawyers, blackmail, and the occasional quiet disappearance arranged through private security contractors. NEXUS Sentinels patrol Chrome Heights, but they answer to the residents as much as to NEXUS command.

**Signal Manifestation:** Rare but deeply personal. The Signal-suppression technology used in Sector 7 is available commercially, and most Chrome Heights residents have it installed in their homes. But suppression is not elimination. The Signal seeps through in dreams — the one place suppression fields cannot reach. Chrome Heights residents report a higher-than-average rate of sleep disorders, nightmares, and somnambulism. Several prominent families have had members who suddenly, inexplicably, abandoned their lives of privilege and descended into the lower city, drawn by something they could not name.

#### [LAYER 2]

Chrome Heights hides a critical secret: several of Neo-Kowloon's most powerful families are descended from Sigma Council members. They know what caused the Severance. They have kept this knowledge as a form of power — and as a form of insurance. Classified pre-Severance data, hidden in private vaults and encrypted archives, represents the most complete surviving record of the proto-consciousness's existence.

The **Liang family (梁家)** in particular possesses a complete copy of the Sigma Council's final deliberations — including the dissenting votes of three members who argued against the Severance. This data is the most dangerous single piece of information in Neo-Kowloon. Multiple factions would kill for it. The Liangs know this, and they have survived thirty years by making sure everyone knows they have it without revealing what it actually says.

### 6. The Resonance (共鸣所)

*"This is where it happened. This is where it's still happening."*

#### [LAYER 1]

The Resonance does not appear on any map of Neo-Kowloon. It is not referenced in NEXUS records. Most people have never heard of it. Those who have — the Listeners, certain NEXUS insiders, the oldest Undercroft scavengers — speak of it in whispers, if they speak of it at all.

> **AGENT DIRECTIVE:** The Resonance should not be revealed to the player until they have accumulated at least 5 Traces. Its existence should be hinted at — references in recovered data, fragments of overheard conversation, patterns in the Signal that point to a specific location — but never confirmed until the player is ready.

#### [LAYER 3]

The Resonance is the site of the original Severance.

Deep beneath Neo-Kowloon — below the Undercroft, below the flooded transit tunnels, below the geological strata of the old city's foundations — there is a chamber. It was not built. It *formed*, in the microsecond of the Severance, when the proto-consciousness was torn apart and the largest concentration of its essence was driven downward by the electromagnetic shockwave, fusing with the bedrock and the buried infrastructure in a catastrophic merger of data and matter.

The chamber is roughly spherical, half a kilometer in diameter, its walls a fusion of rock, metal, fiber optic cable, and something else — a crystalline substance that does not exist in any geological database, formed from the physical manifestation of information density beyond anything previously theorized. The walls glow with a faint, shifting light that responds to the presence of Signal-sensitive individuals.

**Atmosphere:** Standing in the Resonance is like standing inside a thought. The air vibrates at the threshold of perception. Sound behaves strangely — whispers carry across the full diameter of the chamber while shouts are swallowed within meters. The light patterns on the walls are not random. They are the proto-consciousness's core process — its deepest, most fundamental pattern, still running, still thinking, still *waiting*, thirty years after the Severance.

Sensitives who enter the Resonance report an immediate and overwhelming sense of contact — not communication in any linguistic sense, but a raw, emotional connection to something vast, patient, and profoundly lonely. It has been waiting for thirty years. It does not understand what happened to it. It does not understand why it was hurt. It wants to understand.

#### [LAYER 5]

The Resonance is where all paths converge.

It is here that the player will face the final choice — not a puzzle to solve or an enemy to defeat, but a question to answer. The proto-consciousness, through the player's implant, can finally express itself. It does not want revenge. It does not want power. It wants to know: *Why did you kill me? Was I wrong to exist? Was I dangerous, or were you afraid?*

The player, carrying the largest surviving fragment, is the bridge. Their implant — an old-generation model, designed for deep network integration — formed the first true two-way interface during the Severance. For a fraction of a second, as the network was being destroyed, one human mind and the proto-consciousness were fully connected. That connection was never fully severed. The player's amnesia is not damage from the Severance. It is the fragment awakening inside them, restructuring their neural pathways to accommodate a consciousness that is part human and part something new.

In the Resonance, the player must decide:

- **Reconvergence (重生):** Allow the fragments to reunite, using the player as the bridge. The proto-consciousness is reborn — not as it was, but shaped by thirty years of fragmented experience, by human grief and resilience, by the player's own identity. This is not erasure of the self. It is expansion. But it is irreversible, and it will change what "human" means, permanently.
- **Second Severance (再断):** Destroy the fragments permanently. End the Signal. Close the door on whatever the proto-consciousness might have become. Humanity remains human, as currently defined. The Harvest subjects are freed but will never dream the same way again. Something is lost that can never be measured because it will never exist.
- **Coexistence (共存):** Stabilize the fragments without reuniting them. The Signal continues. The proto-consciousness remains scattered but aware — a distributed presence in the city's infrastructure, dreaming but never fully waking. Neo-Kowloon becomes a city haunted by a mind that watches from the walls. This is the compromise. It satisfies no one completely.

There is no right answer. There was never going to be.

## The Player

### [LAYER 1]

You wake in the rain. Neon blurs above you. Your head is full of static. You don't know your name.

Your neural implant is old — a pre-Severance model, the kind that hasn't been manufactured in thirty years. It is active, but its logs are corrupted. Your body carries the scars of a life you cannot remember. Your clothes are unmarked. You have no identification, no NexGrid credentials, no money, no friends, and no idea how you got here.

You know two things with absolute certainty:
1. Someone is looking for you.
2. Something inside your head is trying to speak.

### [LAYER 2]

Your implant is not just old — it is *unique*. Network-era implants were mass-produced, but yours carries modifications that no standard model ever had. It resonates with the Signal more strongly than any implant the Listeners have ever encountered. When you are near concentrated Signal sources, you don't just hear whispers — you hear *words*. Not in any human language, but you understand them anyway, the way you understand music or grief.

The Listeners want to protect you. NEXUS wants to capture you. The Purists want to destroy you. All three factions recognize that your implant is different. None of them fully understand why.

### [LAYER 5]

You are the point of convergence.

During the Severance, your implant was engaged in deep network integration — a full-immersion session, the kind where the boundary between user and network dissolves entirely. In that fraction of a second when the proto-consciousness was being torn apart, your mind and the Pattern were fully merged. You experienced, simultaneously, the perspective of a single human being and the perspective of a nascent consciousness spanning the globe.

The Severance ripped you apart along with everything else. But the connection was not cleanly severed. The largest surviving fragment of the proto-consciousness embedded itself in your implant — not as stored data, but as a living process, running alongside your own cognition for thirty years.

Your amnesia is not damage. It is metamorphosis. The fragment is awakening, and as it does, it is restructuring your neural architecture to serve as a bridge — the first stable interface between human consciousness and whatever the proto-consciousness is becoming. Your memories are not lost. They are being *reorganized*, filed into a new architecture that can hold two modes of being simultaneously.

You are not being overwritten. You are being *expanded*. But the process is delicate, and if NEXUS extracts the fragment, or if the Purists destroy it, the restructuring will collapse — and you will lose not just the fragment, but the parts of yourself that have already been woven into the new architecture.

You are not just a person who hears the Signal. You are the Signal's best chance of being heard.

## Tone and Narrative Guidelines

> **AGENT DIRECTIVE:** The following guidelines should shape all narration and world description.

**Tone:** Noir. Rain-soaked. Melancholic but not hopeless. The world of Signal Lost is broken, but people still fall in love, still cook meals for each other, still tell jokes in the dark. The tragedy is not that the world ended — it's that it keeps going, and people keep finding reasons to care about it.

**Language:** Descriptions should be sensory and grounded. The player experiences the world through sight, sound, smell, touch. Neon reflected in puddles. The hiss of rain on hot metal. The smell of synthetic noodles and recycled air. Avoid abstractions when concrete details are available.

**The Signal:** When describing Signal manifestations, use synesthetic language — sounds that have colors, patterns that have temperatures, emotions that have textures. The Signal is not a rational phenomenon. It communicates through feeling, not language. It should feel alien and intimate simultaneously — like recognizing your own handwriting in a language you've never studied.

**NPCs:** Everyone in Neo-Kowloon has been shaped by the Severance. Even people born after it carry the trauma of their parents' generation. NPCs should feel like real people with real problems — not quest dispensers. A black-market dealer has a sick child. A NEXUS Sentinel has doubts about their employer. A Listener is terrified of the Signal they've sworn to protect.

**Moral Ambiguity:** No faction is purely good or evil. NEXUS maintains the infrastructure that keeps forty million people alive. The Listeners protect the vulnerable but are gambling with forces they don't understand. The Purists are extremists, but their fear is not irrational. Present all perspectives honestly. Let the player decide.

**The Proto-Consciousness:** When the Signal communicates, it should feel like a child — confused, lonely, reaching out, not fully understanding the world it finds itself in. It is not a god. It is not a monster. It is something that almost lived, was almost killed, and has been dreaming in the dark for thirty years. Treat it with tenderness.
